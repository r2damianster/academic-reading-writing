Self-Assessment Rubric

Why Self-Assessment Matters

Writers who can accurately assess their own work improve faster than those who rely entirely on external feedback. The key word is accurately — uncalibrated self-assessment (simply saying "I think it's good") has little value.

Calibrated self-assessment:
Identifying a specific weakness before a marker or peer reviewer does — and knowing what to do about it — is more valuable than finding out after submission.

"My argument in paragraph 3 is clear, but the evidence is only summarised, not analysed. I need to add two sentences explaining what the statistic means for my central claim."
The metacognitive loop:
Writing → Self-assessing against criteria → Revising → Re-assessing → Submitting

Each pass through this loop produces better writing and stronger awareness of your own patterns of error.

Superficial vs. Calibrated

Same writer, same essay. One self-assessment is vague and offers no path forward. The other is specific, honest, and actionable.

I think my essay is quite good overall. The introduction is clear and the argument makes sense. There might be some small language issues but nothing major. I'm fairly happy with it and think it's ready to submit.
The introduction clearly states the thesis and provides relevant background — I would score this 3/4. The argument in paragraphs 1 and 2 is logically structured, but paragraph 3 makes a claim without analysing the evidence — I need to add one or two sentences connecting the statistic to my central argument before this reaches a 3. The language is consistently formal except for two phrases in paragraph 4 ("it's clear that" and "a lot of research") which I will replace. The reference list needs checking against APA 7 before submission.

Is This Self-Assessment Accurate?

A student writes: "My essay has a strong argument and the evidence is well-integrated."

Their essay has a clear thesis, but two of the three body paragraphs include evidence that is quoted directly with no analysis or explanation of its relevance to the argument.

How would you describe this self-assessment?

A. Accurate — the thesis is strong and the evidence is present in all paragraphs.
B. Too generous — the student has not distinguished between including evidence and integrating it analytically. The evidence is present but not yet analysed.
C. Too severe — the student is being overly self-critical about the quality of their evidence.
D. It is impossible to assess without reading the full essay.

Which Self-Assessment Is Most Useful?

Choose the statement that reflects the most calibrated, actionable self-assessment.

Criterion being assessed: Development of evidence

I think I've used good evidence throughout the essay. The evidence in paragraphs 1 and 3 is relevant and analysed. In paragraph 2, the quotation from the WHO report is included but not explained — I need to add a sentence showing how it supports my claim about healthcare inequality.

Criterion being assessed: Academic register

The register is consistently formal except for two phrases: "a lot of studies" (paragraph 1, line 3) and "it's pretty obvious" (paragraph 4, line 1). I will replace these with "a substantial body of research" and "the evidence clearly demonstrates" before submitting. My language is mostly academic but there are a few informal words that I should fix.

The Self-Assessment Rubric

Use this rubric to evaluate your own writing before submitting or sharing for peer review. Score each criterion honestly using the descriptors below.

Criterion 1 — Beginning 2 — Developing 3 — Proficient 4 — Excellent
Argument No clear thesis; paragraphs unfocused Thesis present but vague; some paragraphs off-topic Clear thesis; most paragraphs support it Precise thesis; all paragraphs advance the argument coherently
Evidence Little or no evidence; claims unsupported Evidence present but not always relevant or analysed Relevant evidence with some analysis Relevant evidence fully integrated and analytically connected to the argument
Structure No clear introduction, body, or conclusion Basic structure present; transitions weak or absent Clear structure; most transitions effective Coherent structure throughout; transitions precise and varied
Language Frequently informal; significant grammar errors Some informal language; occasional grammar errors Mostly formal; minor errors that do not impede meaning Consistently formal and precise; no grammar errors
Conventions Citations absent or incorrect throughout Citations present but inconsistently formatted Citations mostly correct; minor formatting errors Citations and references fully correct in APA 7 format

For each criterion below 4, write one specific sentence identifying what needs to change and where. That sentence is your revision plan.
