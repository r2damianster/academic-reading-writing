Relative Clauses

One Comma. Two Different Sentences.

Relative clauses come in two types. The difference is not stylistic — it changes the meaning of the sentence entirely.

DEFINING (Restrictive)

Tells you which one. Removes the clause → the sentence loses its specific meaning. No commas.

"The study that used a control group produced more reliable results."

→ There are multiple studies. This clause identifies the specific one you mean.
NON-DEFINING (Non-restrictive)

Adds extra information about something already identified. Remove it → the core meaning survives. Commas required.

"The Smith et al. study, which used a control group, produced more reliable results."

→ Only one study by Smith et al. The clause adds a detail, not an identification.

The comma test: If removing the relative clause leaves a sentence that is still fully meaningful and refers to the same thing — it is non-defining and needs commas. If removing it makes the sentence vague or ambiguous — it is defining and takes no commas.

Defining or Non-defining?

Read these two sentences carefully. They use identical words but different punctuation. Which statement correctly describes the difference in meaning?

A. "The researchers who had prior fieldwork experience performed significantly better."
B. "The researchers, who had prior fieldwork experience, performed significantly better."

A identifies a specific subgroup of researchers (those with experience). B refers to all the researchers and adds that they all happened to have experience.
Both sentences mean the same thing — the commas in B are optional stylistic choices.
B is more formal than A because it uses additional punctuation.
A implies the researchers lacked experience; B implies they had it.

Five Pronouns, Clear Rules

Choosing the correct relative pronoun is not optional — each has a specific function and set of constraints:

which — things and ideas; non-defining only in strict academic style
"The framework, which was developed in the 1990s, has since been widely adopted."
that — things and ideas; defining only — never in non-defining clauses
"The only variable that showed a significant effect was socioeconomic status."
who / whom — people only; who = subject; whom = object (formal)
"Participants who withdrew were excluded from the final analysis."
"The theorist whom Foucault most directly challenged was Althusser."
whose — possession (people or things)
"Communities whose infrastructure was damaged showed the highest displacement rates."
where / when — place / time (in formal relative clauses)
"This is the period when institutional trust began to collapse."

❌ Never: "The study, that used a control group, showed..." — that cannot introduce a non-defining clause.

Integration vs. Fragmentation

Same information. One version presents it as disconnected short sentences. The other integrates it using relative clauses — the mark of academic density and cohesion.

Sen developed the capability approach. It has been applied in over 200 empirical studies. The approach focuses on human freedoms. These freedoms are both the means and the ends of development. Sen received the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1998.
Sen's capability approach, which focuses on human freedoms as both the means and ends of development, has been applied in over 200 empirical studies. Sen, who received the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1998, developed the framework as a direct challenge to income-based measures of human welfare — measures that, in his view, fundamentally misrepresent what development should achieve.
