Reporting Verbs

A Reporting Verb Is Not Neutral

Every reporting verb encodes three pieces of information simultaneously. Choosing the wrong one distorts the source — and your credibility as a writer.

(1) Epistemic strength — how certain is the claim?
Weak → Strong: implies → suggests → indicates → demonstrates → establishes → proves
Using proves when a study only suggests is misrepresentation.
(2) Writer's attitude toward the source — do you agree, disagree, or stay neutral?
Neutral: states / notes / observes / reports / writes
Positive: demonstrates / confirms / establishes / shows
Tentative: suggests / implies / indicates / proposes
Critical: claims / argues / contends / asserts / alleges
(3) Position in your argument — are you supporting, contrasting, or setting up a critique?
"Smith (2019) demonstrates that X." → you agree, using it as support
"Smith (2019) argues that X." → you may be about to challenge it
"Smith (2019) acknowledges that X." → concession from the source itself

Grammar Patterns: Not All Verbs Work the Same Way

Reporting verbs do not all share the same grammatical structure. Using the wrong pattern is a grammatical error, not just a stylistic one:

Verb + that clause (most common):
✅ argues / suggests / states / demonstrates / acknowledges / claims + that...

Verb + noun phrase:
✅ identifies / examines / analyses / addresses / considers + [noun phrase]
"Chen (2021) identifies three structural barriers to implementation."

Verb + to-infinitive (limited set):
✅ attempts to / seeks to / aims to / fails to / tends to
"The study fails to account for selection bias."

Verb + -ing (limited set):
✅ recommends / advocates / proposes + -ing or that...
"Rawls (1971) advocates prioritising the interests of the least advantaged."

❌ Common errors: "suggests to do" / "argues about that" / "demonstrates about" — these patterns do not exist.

Choose the Right Attitude

Read the context. Choose the reporting verb that correctly encodes the writer's relationship to the source.

Context: You are citing a study whose findings you fully accept as evidence for your argument.

A longitudinal study by Kim et al. (2020) demonstrates claims that early bilingual education produces measurable cognitive advantages that persist into adulthood.

Context: You are about to challenge the theoretical position of this author.

Fukuyama (1992) confirms argues that the end of the Cold War represented the final triumph of liberal democracy — a position that subsequent political developments have significantly complicated.

Context: The source reports a finding but you think the evidence is only preliminary.

The pilot study proves suggests a positive association between mindfulness training and workplace productivity, though the sample size is too small to draw firm conclusions.
