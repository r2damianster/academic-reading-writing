Beyond the Template

In academic writing, most students learn a simple formula:

1. Introduction — Present the topic and thesis.
2. Development — Body arguments and evidence.
3. Conclusion — Synthesis and closing.

While this framework is a useful starting point, university-level writing demands something more sophisticated. Researchers have found that academic essays are not rigid templates — they are flexible tools shaped by communicative purpose: the specific goal a writer wants to achieve in a given text.

Based on: Goulart, L., Biber, D., & Reppen, R. (2022). In this essay, I will…: Examining variation of communicative purpose in student written genres. Journal of English for Academic Purposes, 59.

The Logic of Communicative Purpose

Read the following excerpt carefully before answering the questions below.

Writing scholars and instructors all generally agree that students are required to write many different types of texts, or different genres, as part of a normal university education. These texts must accomplish a range of different communicative purposes — defined as "the primary goal and intention of anyone involved in an act of communication on a given occasion."

Over the last decade, two in-depth studies exploring the communicative purposes of university written genres have been carried out: the Michigan Corpus of Upper-Level Student Papers (MICUSP) and the British Academic Written English (BAWE) Corpus. However, a comparison of these studies shows that there is virtually no consensus on the inventory of particular genres or even on the number of different student writing genres. For example, 'case study' is a genre identified by many researchers, but its defining characteristics range widely.

Goulart, Biber & Reppen (2022), p. 1–2.

Fluidity and Hybridity in Structure

Rather than forcing texts into rigid categories, researchers now embrace the fact that register distinctions are fluid, with fuzzy boundaries and extensive internal variation. This means that texts within the same genre do not necessarily share exactly the same communicative characteristics.

More importantly, research has shown that a single text might be characterized by multiple communicative purposes. Individual essays can combine purposes like persuading, comparing, or analyzing — often within the same paper. The clearest example of this is the diversity among student essays: some papers argue for a point of view, others compare different areas, and others analyze an object of study.

Goulart, Biber & Reppen (2022), p. 3–4.

Conclusions on Essay Architecture

The analysis has clearly shown that essays, critiques, and case studies show extensive variation in the major purpose of texts, to the extent that any generalization about the typical communicative purposes of those genres would be suspect. In addition, those three genres show considerable overlap in major purpose.

The results reveal that most texts contain more than one communicative purpose. For example, 55.7% of essays combine two communicative purposes, while 56.1% of critiques combine three communicative purposes. This study highlights that academic writing is not just about following a template, but about the strategic combination of rhetorical goals. Understanding this complexity is essential for students to move beyond the 'In this essay, I will...' cliché and develop a sophisticated awareness of how their writing choices shape their academic work.

Goulart, Biber & Reppen (2022), p. 8–9.
