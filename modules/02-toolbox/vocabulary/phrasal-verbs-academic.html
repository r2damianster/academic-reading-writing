Phrasal Verbs in Academic Writing

Replace or Keep?

Phrasal verbs do not disappear from academic English — they divide into two groups. Confusing these groups produces either informal writing or unnecessarily awkward paraphrasing.

❌ REPLACE in academic writing

These carry informal register and must be replaced with a single-word equivalent:

find out → determine / discover
look at → examine / analyse
come up with → develop / propose
point out → indicate / highlight
go up → increase / rise
go down → decrease / decline
get rid of → eliminate / remove
set up → establish / create
✅ KEEP — acceptable in academic writing

These are standard in academic texts and have no exact single-word equivalent:

carry out (research / a study)
draw on (evidence / theory)
account for (a factor / a finding)
build on (previous work)
give rise to (consequences)
set out (to do / an argument)
point to (evidence / a conclusion)
result in (an outcome)

Five You Must Use Actively

These five academically acceptable phrasal verbs are highly productive — they appear in a wide range of contexts and have no exact single-word equivalent that carries the same meaning:

carry out — to perform or conduct a process
✅ "The researchers carried out a series of semi-structured interviews."
Collocates with: research / a study / an investigation / an analysis / a review
draw on — to use a source, theory, or body of evidence as a basis
✅ "This analysis draws on Foucault's concept of discourse."
Collocates with: theory / evidence / the work of / previous research / multiple frameworks
account for — to explain or constitute a portion of something
✅ "Socioeconomic background accounts for a significant proportion of the variance."
✅ "The model fails to account for seasonal variation."
build on — to develop or extend existing work
✅ "This paper builds on the theoretical framework established by Sen (1999)."
Collocates with: previous work / existing research / the findings of / earlier models
give rise to — to cause or produce a consequence
✅ "Rapid urbanisation has given rise to a range of social and environmental challenges."
Collocates with: concerns / complications / inequality / new frameworks / debate

The study looked at how income inequality affects educational attainment across three national contexts. The study examined how income inequality affects educational attainment across three national contexts.

Order the Methods Paragraph

These five sentences form a methods paragraph. Each uses an academically appropriate phrasal verb. Put them in the correct procedural order.

This study builds on existing research into the relationship between housing tenure and mental health outcomes.
Drawing on a mixed-methods design, the study carried out both a quantitative survey and a series of qualitative interviews.
It draws on data from the UK Household Longitudinal Study, supplemented by primary qualitative data collected between 2021 and 2022.
The analysis accounts for a range of socioeconomic confounders, including income, employment status, and neighbourhood deprivation.
The quantitative component set out to identify predictors of mental health deterioration among private renters aged 25–45.

Writing Task Instructions

You will write a methodology paragraph that uses academically appropriate phrasal verbs correctly, while avoiding informal ones. The challenge is to use the five acceptable academic phrasal verbs naturally — not forced — within a coherent academic paragraph. Target length: 100–130 words. Allow approximately 10 minutes. Your writing will be tracked for academic integrity purposes.

Write a methodology paragraph (100–130 words) for a hypothetical study on the following topic:

"The impact of social media use on political engagement among university students."

Requirements:
— Use at least three of the five academic phrasal verbs from this lesson (carry out / draw on / account for / build on / give rise to)
— Use zero informal phrasal verbs — replace any that appear with their formal equivalents
— Use each academic phrasal verb with its correct collocation (carry out + a study/survey/analysis, draw on + theory/evidence/data, etc.)
— Maintain impersonal academic register throughout — no first person
