How to Receive Feedback

Receiving Feedback Is an Active Skill

Reading feedback and deciding what to do with it requires judgement. There are three legitimate responses — and all three are sometimes correct:

(1) Accept — the reviewer identified a genuine problem and their suggested fix works

Feedback: "Your topic sentence makes two claims."
Response: Revise the topic sentence to focus on one claim. ✅
(2) Adapt — the reviewer identified a real problem, but their suggested solution does not fit your argument; you find a different solution

Feedback: "Add more evidence to paragraph 2."
Response: The evidence is sufficient — the real problem is the analysis is too brief. You expand the explanation instead. ✅
(3) Reject with argument — the reviewer misunderstood your intention; you can defend your choice with a clear reason

Feedback: "Your conclusion is too short."
Response: The conclusion is intentionally concise — the discipline convention for this type of paper favours brevity. You keep it and note the reason. ✅

The writer always makes the final decision. Feedback is information from a reader — not an instruction from an authority.

What Is the Best Response?

A reviewer writes: "The transition between paragraph 2 and paragraph 3 is abrupt — you should add a however or nevertheless."

The writer reads both paragraphs and agrees the transition is abrupt, but feels that adding however would misrepresent the logical relationship — the second paragraph extends the first, not contrasts it. What should the writer do?

A. Accept — the reviewer is right, add however as suggested.
B. Adapt — the reviewer correctly identified the transition problem, but the solution should be a connector like furthermore or a bridging sentence, not however.
C. Reject — the reviewer does not understand the argument, ignore the comment entirely.
D. Ask the teacher to decide instead of making a judgement.

How Should the Writer Respond?

Drag each writer's response to the correct category.

The reviewer said the thesis was unclear. The writer rewrites it to state a clear position.
The reviewer noted a spelling error in the title. The writer corrects it immediately.
The reviewer said "add more examples." The writer decides the problem is actually a lack of analysis, not examples, and expands the explanation instead.
The reviewer suggested cutting paragraph 4 entirely. The writer keeps the content but condenses it to two sentences and integrates it into paragraph 3.
The reviewer said the essay should not use passive voice. The writer is writing a methods section where passive is the disciplinary convention and keeps it.
The reviewer disagreed with the writer's argument on ideological grounds. The writer keeps the argument, noting that disagreement is not the same as a structural flaw.

What Feedback Is Not

Three misunderstandings about peer feedback that make it harder to use productively:

❌ Not a personal judgement
Feedback about the writing is not feedback about you as a person or a thinker. "The argument is underdeveloped" means the text needs more work — it says nothing about your intelligence or effort.
❌ Not a mandatory instruction
A reviewer who says "you should restructure the whole essay" is offering a perspective, not an order. You evaluate the suggestion against your own understanding of the argument and decide.
❌ Not expert opinion
Your peer reviewer is a reader, not an authority. Their feedback is valuable because it tells you how a real reader experienced your text — not because they are necessarily right about what you should do.

The Most Professional Response

Choose the response that shows the writer is engaging with feedback productively and professionally.

Feedback received: "I don't think your argument about social media is convincing."

The writer responds: "You just don't agree with my opinion, that's not real feedback." The writer responds: "Thank you — could you point to where the argument felt weakest? That would help me identify whether it's a structural issue or an evidence issue."

Feedback received: "The conclusion is too long and repeats too much."

The writer re-reads the conclusion, agrees it restates evidence unnecessarily, and cuts two sentences while keeping the synthesis and broader implications. The writer accepts all the feedback and deletes the entire conclusion, replacing it with one sentence.

Feedback received: "You should not use the word 'however' so much."

The writer removes every instance of however from the essay regardless of whether it is the right connector in each case. The writer re-reads each instance: keeps the ones where contrast is the correct logical relationship, and replaces others with more precise connectors where appropriate.
