Organizational Patterns

Five Ways to Organize a Paragraph

The content of a paragraph is not enough — the order in which you present it shapes how convincing your argument is. There are five core organizational patterns:

  • General → Specific — Start with a broad claim, then narrow it down with evidence. The most common pattern in academic writing.
  • Specific → General — Start with a concrete detail or case, then draw a wider conclusion. More narrative in tone.
  • Chronological — Arrange information in time order. Best for historical events or processes with clear stages.
  • Problem → Solution — Identify a problem first, then propose or evaluate a solution. Common in argumentative writing.
  • Claim → Concession → Refutation — State your claim, acknowledge the opposing view, then argue why your position is stronger. The most sophisticated pattern.

Same Content, Different Pattern

Both paragraphs use the same facts about plastic pollution. Notice how the pattern changes the argumentative effect.

Claim: Single-use plastics represent the most urgent category of environmental pollution today.
Evidence: A 2023 UN report found that 380 million tonnes of plastic are produced annually, of which 91% is never recycled.
Analysis: This accumulation, concentrated in marine ecosystems, constitutes a systemic rather than isolated threat.
Case: A sea turtle found off the coast of Costa Rica had 104 pieces of plastic in its stomach.
Broader data: Similar findings have been documented in 700 marine species globally.
Conclusion: Single-use plastics therefore represent a major environmental crisis.

The Sophisticated Pattern

However, research consistently shows that students in bilingual programmes outperform monolingual peers in standardized literacy assessments by the age of ten.
Bilingual education produces stronger long-term academic outcomes than monolingual instruction.
