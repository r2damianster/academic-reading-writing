Discipline-Specific Vocabulary

Not All Academic Words Are Equal

When you read an academic text, three distinct vocabulary layers are operating at the same time. Recognising which layer a word belongs to tells you how to learn it and when to use it.

(1) General Academic Vocabulary (AWL)
Appears across all disciplines. Transferable. High frequency in academic texts.
analyse / framework / variable / assumption / demonstrate / constitute / assess / indicate
→ Learn these first. They work everywhere.
(2) Semi-technical Vocabulary
Common words with a discipline-specific meaning that differs from everyday use.
culture (sociology ≠ biology) / register (linguistics ≠ music) / capital (economics ≠ sociology) / depression (psychology ≠ geology)
→ These are the most dangerous — you recognise the word but may misread its meaning.
(3) Technical Vocabulary
Discipline-specific terminology with no common-language equivalent.
heteroscedasticity (statistics) / habitus (sociology) / apoptosis (biology) / episteme (philosophy)
→ Use only when necessary. Define on first use. Never use to impress.

How to Extract Vocabulary From a Text

Passive reading does not build active vocabulary. This four-step protocol turns any dense academic text into a vocabulary acquisition tool:

Step 1 — Identify the term
Mark every word you do not know or that seems to carry special weight in context.
Step 2 — Find the operational definition
Academic writers almost always define key terms within the first few uses. Look for "X, defined as..." / "X refers to..." / "by X, I mean..." — or for a contrasting term that implicitly defines by opposition.
Step 3 — Identify the core collocation
What verb or adjective most frequently accompanies this noun? What noun follows this verb? The collocation is more useful than the isolated word.
Step 4 — Find the contrasting term
Most academic concepts are defined in opposition to something: qualitative / quantitative, macro / micro, synchronic / diachronic. Knowing the opposite anchors the meaning.

Bourdieu's concept of habitus refers to the durable, transposable dispositions that individuals acquire through socialisation and that structure both perception and practice. This framework constitutes a significant contribution to the analysis of how social structures are reproduced through individual behaviour, and has been widely applied across diverse disciplinary contexts. The field, as Bourdieu conceives it, is a structured space of positions defined by the distribution of capital — economic, cultural, and social. Intersectionality, as developed by Crenshaw (1989), insists that social categories such as race, class, and gender cannot be understood in isolation but must be analysed as mutually constitutive systems of oppression.

Society is shaped by the unequal distribution of resources between individuals and groups.
Social stratification refers to the hierarchical arrangement of individuals and groups within a society based on their access to valued resources.
In Weberian terms, stratification is determined by the intersection of class (economic position), status (social honour), and party (political power).
Bourdieu reconceptualises stratification through the notion of field-specific capital, arguing that position within a field is determined by the volume and composition of economic, cultural, and social capital an agent possesses.
