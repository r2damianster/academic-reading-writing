Hedging Vocabulary

The Hedging Lexicon — Organised by Function

The grammar module covered how hedging works structurally. This lesson covers the specific expressions you need to deploy — organised not by grammar type but by what job they do in the sentence.

(1) Limiting scope — restricts the claim to specific conditions
in most cases / under certain conditions / in this context / to a certain extent / within the scope of this study / in the majority of instances
(2) Expressing possibility — signals the claim is inferred, not proven
it is possible that / there is reason to believe / the evidence suggests / this may be explained by / a plausible interpretation is / it appears that
(3) Attributing to source — distances the claim from the writer
according to / as argued by / in the view of / as reported by / drawing on the work of / consistent with the findings of
(4) Acknowledging limitation — signals awareness of what the evidence cannot prove
it should be noted that / a caveat is that / this study is limited by / it must be acknowledged that / one limitation of this approach is / the present findings cannot be generalised to

Hedging Collocations: The Patterns That Matter

Hedging expressions do not stand alone — they collocate with specific verbs, nouns, and adjectives. Getting these wrong signals a non-native writer immediately:

suggest collocates with:
✅ the evidence suggests that / the data suggests a / findings suggest a relationship
❌ the evidence suggests to do / the data suggests about

indicate collocates with:
✅ the results indicate that / the analysis indicates a significant
❌ the results indicate about / the findings indicate of

evidence collocates with:
✅ evidence suggests / evidence indicates / evidence supports / there is evidence to suggest
❌ evidence demonstrates (too strong) / evidence proves (too strong for hedged context)

appear / seem: always followed by to + infinitive
✅ the pattern appears to reflect / the data seems to suggest
❌ the pattern appears that / the data seems that

Calibrate the Expression

Each context describes a specific evidence situation. Choose the hedging expression that best matches the strength of the evidence.

Context: A single qualitative case study — cannot generalise, only interpret.

The evidence demonstrates that institutional trust is declining across all democratic systems. Within the scope of this single case, a plausible interpretation is that institutional trust is eroding in response to perceived democratic failures.

Context: A meta-analysis of 60 studies, consistent effect across all of them.

The evidence strongly suggests a robust association between early childhood intervention and long-term educational attainment. It is possible that early childhood intervention might perhaps be associated with educational outcomes in some cases.

Context: You are citing the position of a specific researcher you are about to challenge.

The evidence proves that markets self-regulate, as all economists agree. As argued by Friedman (1962), markets tend to self-regulate under conditions of sufficient competition — a claim that subsequent empirical research has significantly complicated.

Formal Language | Academic Workspace

Formal Language

Register Is a System, Not a Word List

Formal academic register is not achieved by swapping informal words for longer ones. It involves four simultaneous choices that must all point in the same direction:

(1) Lexis — choose precise, Latinate vocabulary over Anglo-Saxon informality
get → obtain / show → demonstrate / think → argue / use → employ / look at → examine
(2) Person — avoid first person unless your discipline explicitly requires it
❌ "In this essay I will argue..." → ✅ "This essay argues..."
❌ "We can see that..." → ✅ "The data demonstrates that..."
(3) Contractions — never in academic writing
❌ don't / it's / can't / won't / there's
✅ do not / it is / cannot / will not / there is
(4) Vague intensifiers — do not exist in academic writing
❌ a lot of / very important / really significant / big problem / sort of / kind of
✅ a substantial number of / particularly significant / notably important / considerable challenge

Three Traps That Fool Writers

Knowing that you need formal language is not enough. These three traps catch writers who think they are being formal:

Trap 1 — False formality (utilize problem)
Utilize sounds more formal than use — but in most contexts they mean the same thing and use is preferred. Using a longer word is not automatically better: commence for begin, endeavour for try, aforementioned for the above often read as pompous, not formal.
Trap 2 — Hidden first person
Avoiding I and we is not enough. These constructions still carry informal personal voice:
❌ "In this essay, it will be shown that..." (passive but still essay-centric)
❌ "As the reader can see..." (addresses reader directly)
✅ "The evidence demonstrates that..." / "This analysis reveals..."
Trap 3 — Informal intensifiers in disguise
These read as informal even though they seem neutral:
❌ very / quite / fairly / pretty / rather + adjective (hedges uncertainty without precision)
✅ considerably / substantially / markedly / notably / significantly + adjective

Choose the Formal Version

In each case, one option is appropriate for academic writing; the other is not.

I think that the data shows a pretty clear link between poverty and educational outcomes. The data suggests a substantial association between socioeconomic disadvantage and educational attainment.

This paper examines the relationship between institutional trust and civic participation across twenty OECD countries. In this paper, I'm going to look at how trust in institutions affects civic participation in twenty OECD countries.

It can't be denied that the policy didn't work as well as expected. It cannot be denied that the policy failed to achieve its projected outcomes.

The findings demonstrate a statistically significant reduction in recidivism rates among participants who completed the programme. The findings show a really significant drop in reoffending rates for people who finished the programme.

This paragraph contains a sentence with a clear register violation — either a contraction, an informal expression, or a first-person intrusion that breaks academic register. Click on that sentence.

The relationship between economic inequality and health outcomes has been extensively documented in the epidemiological literature. Research consistently demonstrates that individuals in lower socioeconomic groups experience disproportionately higher rates of chronic illness, reduced life expectancy, and limited access to preventive care. I think it's really important to point out that these inequalities aren't just about money — they're about power and access too. The structural determinants of health — including housing quality, occupational safety, and educational attainment — operate as compounding mechanisms that amplify the effects of income inequality over time. Addressing these inequalities therefore requires systemic intervention rather than individual-level behavioural change.

The Full Register Shift

Same argument, completely different register. Notice that the shift is not just lexical — person, contractions, sentence structure, and precision all change simultaneously.

I'm going to talk about how social media affects mental health in young people. There's a lot of research that shows it can be really bad for teenagers, especially girls. But we can't just blame the apps — it's more complicated than that. I think we need to look at the bigger picture and think about what's really going on.
This paper examines the relationship between social media use and mental health outcomes among adolescents. A substantial body of research demonstrates that intensive platform engagement is associated with elevated rates of anxiety and depression, particularly among female users. However, these associations cannot be attributed to platform design alone; the broader socioeconomic and relational context in which adolescents engage with social media must also be considered.
