Academic Collocations

Words Travel in Pairs

A collocation is a pair or group of words that habitually occur together. In academic English, collocations are not optional preferences — using the wrong combination marks you immediately as a non-native academic writer, even when your grammar is perfect.

(1) Verb + Noun
✅ conduct research / carry out a study / draw conclusions / raise questions / address limitations
❌ make research / do conclusions / say questions
(2) Adjective + Noun
✅ significant increase / substantial evidence / robust findings / compelling argument / theoretical framework
❌ big increase / strong evidence (ambiguous) / convincing findings (register mismatch)
(3) Noun + Noun
✅ research framework / policy implications / theoretical approach / data analysis / literature review
(4) Adverb + Adjective
✅ statistically significant / widely accepted / broadly consistent / increasingly evident / deeply problematic
(5) Verb + Adverb
✅ argue convincingly / demonstrate clearly / suggest strongly / differ significantly / contribute substantially

Which Collocation Is Correct?

One option uses a natural academic collocation; the other is grammatically possible but lexically wrong.

The study made a conclusion that urban poverty is worsening. The study drew the conclusion that urban poverty is worsening.

The results demonstrate a statistically significant difference between the two groups. The results demonstrate a mathematically significant difference between the two groups.

These findings strongly argue that the current policy framework requires reform. These findings strongly suggest that the current policy framework requires reform.

Broken vs. Correct

Same paragraph, same argument. One version has broken collocations throughout — grammatically correct but lexically wrong. The other uses natural academic collocations. The difference is immediately felt by any academic reader.

This paper makes a research into the factors that influence policy compliance. The study does an analysis of data from forty municipalities and makes conclusions about the relationship between institutional capacity and implementation success. The findings say strongly that resource allocation constitutes the most important factor. These results do important questions about the equity of current funding models.
This paper conducts a systematic investigation into the factors that influence policy compliance. The study carries out an analysis of data from forty municipalities and draws conclusions about the relationship between institutional capacity and implementation success. The findings suggest strongly that resource allocation constitutes the most significant determinant of outcomes. These results raise important questions about the equity implications of current funding models.

Writing Task Instructions

You will write a results and discussion paragraph demonstrating accurate use of academic collocations from at least three of the five patterns practised. Collocational accuracy — not vocabulary range — is the primary criterion. Target length: 110–130 words. Allow approximately 10 minutes. Your writing will be tracked for academic integrity purposes.

Write a results and discussion paragraph (110–130 words) reporting the findings of the following hypothetical study:

A study examining the impact of a 12-week reading intervention on literacy outcomes in primary school children. The intervention group showed a 34% improvement in reading fluency. The control group showed 8% improvement. The difference was statistically significant. The researchers believe the structured approach to phonics was the key factor.

Your paragraph must include collocations from at least three of the five patterns:
— Verb + Noun: conduct / carry out / draw / raise / address + appropriate noun
— Adjective + Noun: significant / substantial / robust / compelling + appropriate noun
— Adverb + Adjective: statistically significant / broadly consistent / widely accepted
— Verb + Adverb: suggest strongly / demonstrate clearly / differ significantly
— Noun + Noun: research findings / policy implications / theoretical framework
