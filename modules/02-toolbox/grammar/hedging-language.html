Hedging Language

Precision, Not Weakness

Hedging is not uncertainty — it is epistemic precision. Academic writers hedge to calibrate claims to the strength of the evidence behind them. Overclaiming and underclaiming are both errors.

(1) Modal verbs — calibrate the degree of certainty
"This may suggest a causal relationship." / "The results could be explained by..."
(2) Approximators — limit the scope of a quantity or frequency
"Approximately 60% of participants..." / "In most cases..." / "Around half of the studies..."
(3) Distancing verbs — present the claim as observed or inferred, not stated as fact
"The data suggests..." / "Participants appeared to..." / "The pattern tends to..."
(4) Reporting structures — attribute the claim to the evidence or field, not the writer
"It has been argued that..." / "It is suggested that..." / "There is evidence to suggest that..."

The Certainty Scale

Not all modal verbs hedge to the same degree. Choosing the wrong modal misrepresents the strength of your evidence:

⬆️
will — certain prediction (use only when the evidence is conclusive)
"Higher temperatures will increase evaporation rates."
↕️
would — conditional / hypothetical
"This would suggest a structural explanation rather than an individual one."
↕️
may — possible, evidence exists but is not conclusive
"Extended screen time may contribute to attention difficulties."
⬇️
might / could — speculative, weak or preliminary evidence
"These patterns might reflect underlying socioeconomic factors not captured in the dataset."

⚠️ Should is a modal of obligation, not uncertainty — "The results should be interpreted carefully" means "you ought to", not "they possibly are". Do not use it as a hedge.

Overclaimed

This conclusions paragraph contains a sentence where the writer states a finding with more certainty than the evidence supports — no hedging at all where hedging is clearly required. Click on that sentence.

The present study examined the relationship between parental involvement and academic resilience in secondary school students. The findings suggest that higher levels of structured parental engagement are associated with stronger academic recovery following periods of disruption. Parental involvement is the most important factor in determining educational outcomes, and schools that ignore this do so at significant cost to their students. These results are broadly consistent with the existing literature, although the effect sizes observed here were somewhat larger than those reported in comparable studies. Future research might usefully examine whether these associations hold across different cultural contexts and socioeconomic groups.

Too Much Hedge

Hedging can also go wrong in the other direction. Excessive hedging signals a writer who lacks confidence in their own analysis. Calibration means hedging the right claims to the right degree — not hedging everything.

It might possibly be suggested that there could perhaps be some association between what might be considered educational disadvantage and what appears to potentially be lower academic outcomes, although it is of course possible that this may not always be the case in every possible context.
The evidence consistently suggests a strong association between socioeconomic disadvantage and reduced academic attainment. While the magnitude of this relationship may vary across contexts, its direction is well-established across decades of research. What remains less clear is the precise mechanism through which disadvantage operates — whether primarily through material deprivation, cultural capital, or institutional bias.

Writing Task Instructions

You will write a discussion paragraph for a given study, demonstrating calibrated use of all four hedging mechanisms. You must avoid both overclaiming and overhedging — the goal is epistemic precision. Target length: 120–150 words. Allow approximately 12 minutes. Your writing will be tracked for academic integrity purposes.

Write a discussion paragraph (120–150 words) interpreting the following hypothetical findings:

"A study of 340 university students found that those who took handwritten notes scored, on average, 12% higher on recall tests than those who typed. The difference was statistically significant (p < 0.05) but the effect size was moderate (d = 0.43). The study was conducted over one semester at a single institution."

Requirements — use all four hedging mechanisms:
— At least one modal verb calibrated to the strength of the evidence (may / might / could / would)
— At least one approximator (approximately / around / in most cases / tends to)
— At least one distancing verb (suggest / appear / seem / indicate)
— At least one reporting structure (it has been argued that / there is evidence to suggest / it is possible that)
— Do not overclaim: acknowledge the limitations of a single-institution study
