From Paragraphs to Essay

An Essay Is Not a Collection

A common mistake is treating an essay as a series of independent paragraphs — each one complete in itself, loosely grouped under a title. That produces a document, not an argument.

A well-written essay is a single, unified argument in which every part serves the whole:

📌 Introduction (≈10%) — opens the argument and announces the thesis
   ↳ Body Paragraph 1 (≈27%) — develops argument 1
   ↳ Body Paragraph 2 (≈27%) — develops argument 2
   ↳ Body Paragraph 3 (≈26%) — develops argument 3
📌 Conclusion (≈10%) — restates, summarises, and closes

The proportions are a guide, not a rule — but if your introduction is longer than your body, something is structurally wrong.

The Thesis Is the Map

The thesis statement does not just state an opinion — it maps the entire essay. Every body paragraph must be traceable back to one point announced in the thesis.

This creates a test you can apply to any essay:

  1. Read the thesis. Note its 3 points.
  2. Read the first sentence of each body paragraph.
  3. Each topic sentence must match exactly one thesis point.
  4. If a topic sentence introduces a point the thesis never mentioned → coherence failure.
  5. If a thesis point has no corresponding body paragraph → structural gap.

Cohesion means the thesis and the topic sentences tell the same story.

Build the Essay Skeleton

Thesis: "The widespread adoption of electric vehicles will reduce air pollution, lower long-term transport costs, and decrease dependence on fossil fuels."

Drag each Topic Sentence to the thesis point it develops. One sentence is an intruder — it does not belong.

"Electric vehicles produce zero direct emissions, significantly improving air quality in urban areas."
"Although EVs have higher upfront prices, fuel and maintenance savings generate net savings within five years."
"Electrifying transport reduces the volume of oil a country must import, strengthening its energy independence."

Find the Intruder

Thesis: "Universal basic income could reduce poverty, simplify the welfare system, and encourage entrepreneurship."

Which of these Topic Sentences does NOT belong in this essay?

A. "By providing a guaranteed income floor, UBI would lift millions of households above the poverty line."
B. "A single unconditional payment would replace dozens of overlapping benefit programmes, reducing administrative costs."
C. "Critics of UBI argue that it would reduce the incentive to work and increase inflation."
D. "With a financial safety net guaranteed, individuals would be more willing to leave stable jobs to start businesses."

Transitions Between Paragraphs

Connectors like however, furthermore, therefore work within a sentence. But moving from one paragraph to the next requires something larger — a transition sentence that signals a shift in argument.

There are two places where inter-paragraph transitions appear:

  • At the end of a body paragraph — the closing sentence hints at what comes next (covered in Lesson 7)
  • At the start of the next body paragraph — the topic sentence can briefly acknowledge the previous point before introducing the new one
Example bridge opening:
"While the economic benefits of remote work are clear, its impact on employee well-being is equally significant."

This sentence closes the economic argument and opens the well-being argument in one move.

Connected or Disconnected?

Paragraph 1 ends with: "Therefore, regular physical activity significantly reduces the risk of cardiovascular disease."

Paragraph 2 begins with: "Sleep deprivation has become a widespread public health concern in modern societies."

Is the transition between these two paragraphs coherent?

A. No — there is an abrupt jump. The reader cannot see how the two arguments relate.
B. Yes — both topics relate to health, so the connection is obvious.

Where Does the Essay Break?

This is the opening sentence of each paragraph in an essay about the benefits of bilingual education. Click on the Topic Sentence that creates an abrupt jump — it has no connection to the previous argument.

Introduction ends: "...making bilingual education one of the most effective investments a society can make in its children." BP1: "The most well-documented benefit of bilingual education is its positive effect on cognitive flexibility and problem-solving ability." BP2: "Beyond cognitive gains, bilingual students consistently demonstrate stronger academic performance across all subjects, not only in language classes." BP3: "The history of language policy in Latin America has been shaped by colonial and post-colonial political decisions." Conclusion: "In summary, the evidence confirms that bilingual education produces lasting cognitive, academic, and social advantages."

One Essay or Three Fragments?

Both versions discuss the same topic. One reads as a unified argument; the other feels like three separate assignments stapled together.

BP1 ends: "Therefore, social media negatively affects teenagers' mental health."
BP2 opens: "Academic performance is an important factor in a student's future."
→ No bridge. The reader must guess the connection.

BP2 ends: "Thus, grades have declined among heavy social media users."
BP3 opens: "Friendship is a fundamental human need."
→ Another abrupt jump. Three isolated ideas, not one argument.
BP1 ends: "...confirming that social media overuse poses a direct risk to adolescent psychological health."
BP2 opens: "This psychological toll does not remain confined to emotional well-being — it spills directly into academic performance."
→ BP2 picks up where BP1 left off.

BP2 ends: "...suggesting that the academic damage is both measurable and preventable."
BP3 opens: "The consequences extend further still: the quality of teenagers' real-world social relationships is also being reshaped."
→ The essay builds progressively.

Writing Task Instructions

You will write the structural skeleton of a complete essay — not the full essay, but its load-bearing elements: the thesis, the three topic sentences, and the restatement. This task tests whether you can design a coherent argument before writing it in full. Allow approximately 8–10 minutes. Your writing will be tracked for academic integrity purposes.

Topic: "The impact of artificial intelligence on the future of work."

Write the essay skeleton — not the full essay. Your response must include exactly these four elements, clearly labelled:

Thesis: One sentence announcing your position and three supporting arguments.
Topic Sentence 1: Opens Body Paragraph 1, responds to argument 1.
Topic Sentence 2: Opens Body Paragraph 2, includes a bridge from TS1.
Topic Sentence 3: Opens Body Paragraph 3, includes a bridge from TS2.
Restatement: Paraphrase of the thesis in different words.

Each sentence should be complete and academic. Total: approximately 5 sentences.
