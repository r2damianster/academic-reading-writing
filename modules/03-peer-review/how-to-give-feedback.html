How to Give Feedback

What Makes Feedback Useful?

Not all feedback helps the writer improve. Useful academic feedback has three qualities that work together:

(1) Specific — points to exactly what and where

❌ "This paragraph is confusing."
✅ "The topic sentence in paragraph 2 introduces two separate claims. The reader loses track of which one you are developing."
(2) Actionable — the writer knows exactly what to do next

❌ "Your argument needs to be stronger."
✅ "The evidence in paragraph 3 supports the claim but is not analysed — try explaining what the statistic means for your argument."
(3) Balanced — acknowledges what works, not just what doesn't

❌ "There are many problems with this essay."
✅ "Your introduction is clear and well-structured. The main area to develop is the analysis of evidence in the body paragraphs."

Vague vs. Useful

Same observation, two ways of expressing it. One leaves the writer uncertain what to do. The other gives them a clear next step.

This essay has some good points but the argument is not very clear. The paragraphs need more development and the conclusion could be better. The language is sometimes too informal. Overall it needs more work before it is ready to submit.
Your thesis statement clearly identifies the problem, which gives the essay a strong starting point. In paragraph 2, the topic sentence makes two separate claims — focusing on one would make the argument easier to follow. The evidence in paragraph 3 (the WHO statistic) is relevant but not analysed; a sentence explaining what it means for your argument would strengthen the paragraph significantly. The conclusion restates the thesis but does not address the broader implications — consider adding one sentence connecting your findings to the wider debate.

Which Comment Is Most Useful?

A student has written a body paragraph about the causes of urban poverty. Which reviewer comment is most useful?

A. "This paragraph is quite good but could be improved."
B. "You need to add more evidence and analysis to make your argument stronger."
C. "Your topic sentence clearly states the claim. The evidence from the UN report (line 4) supports it well, but the explanation stops there — the reader needs one more sentence connecting this evidence back to your central argument about structural inequality."
D. "I don't think this argument works. Urban poverty is much more complex than this."

Specific or Vague?

Choose the comment that is specific, actionable, and appropriate in tone.

Situation: The writer's introduction lacks a clear thesis statement.

The introduction is not very good and doesn't really say what the essay is about. The introduction provides relevant background context, but the final sentence does not state your position clearly — the reader is not sure what argument the essay will make. A one-sentence thesis at the end of the introduction would solve this.

Situation: The writer uses informal language in an academic essay.

In paragraph 2, phrases like "a lot of people think" and "it's really obvious that" reduce the academic register. Replacing these with "a significant proportion of the population" and "the evidence clearly demonstrates" would bring the language in line with academic expectations. Your language is too informal throughout. You need to use more academic words.

Situation: The conclusion simply repeats the introduction word for word.

The conclusion is basically the same as the introduction, which is a problem. The conclusion restates the thesis almost verbatim from the introduction. It would be stronger if it synthesised the evidence developed in the body paragraphs and pointed toward the broader implications of your argument rather than repeating the opening.

The Language of Constructive Feedback

How you say something matters as much as what you say. These patterns signal that feedback is a suggestion from a reader — not a correction from an authority:

Suggesting rather than instructing:
✅ "You might consider..." / "It could be worth..." / "One option would be..."

Framing as reader experience:
✅ "The reader may benefit from..." / "At this point, I was unsure whether..." / "I found it difficult to follow..."

Acknowledging alternatives:
✅ "It would strengthen the argument if..." / "This might be clearer if..."

Separating observation from judgement:
✅ "The paragraph currently does X — if you want to achieve Y, you might try Z."

The goal of peer feedback is to give the writer more information about how their text is being read — not to rewrite it for them or to evaluate their effort.
