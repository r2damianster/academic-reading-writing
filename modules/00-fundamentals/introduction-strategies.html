Introduction Strategies

The Introduction Has Three Jobs

An introduction is not just "the beginning." Each of its three parts does a specific rhetorical job:

🎣 Hook — grabs attention and creates a reason to keep reading
🌍 Background — contextualises the topic so the thesis makes sense
📌 Thesis — states the argument the essay will defend

The movement is deliberate: from broad (hook) to specific (thesis). The thesis almost always appears as the last sentence of the introduction — this is the academic standard.

A common mistake is putting the thesis first and then explaining it. That inverts the funnel and weakens the rhetorical effect.

Does This Hook Work?

Evaluate this opening sentence:

"Since the beginning of time, human beings have always been interested in education and learning new things."

A. Effective — it establishes a universal context for the essay.
B. Too general — it says nothing specific and could open any essay on any topic.

Weak vs. Strong Hook

Both hooks open an essay about the mental health effects of social media. One earns the reader's attention; the other wastes it.

"Many people think that social media is an important part of modern life and that it has both advantages and disadvantages."

Why it fails: vague opinion, no specificity, tells the reader nothing they don't already know.
"In 2023, the U.S. Surgeon General issued an official advisory warning that social media poses a 'profound risk' to adolescent mental health — the first time a public health authority has used such language about a digital platform."

Why it works: specific, surprising, raises a question, makes the reader want to know more.

Assemble the Introduction

These three sentences form a complete introduction. Drag them into the correct order: Hook → Background → Thesis.

This essay argues that universities must restructure their curricula to include mandatory digital literacy training, establish clear usage guidelines, and invest in mental health support services tailored to social media-related distress.
In 2023, the U.S. Surgeon General issued an advisory warning that social media poses a profound risk to adolescent mental health — the first time a public health authority has used such language about a digital platform.
As young people spend an average of seven hours per day on screens, universities are confronting a generation of students whose psychological well-being has been shaped, and in many cases damaged, by digital environments before they arrive on campus.

Three Hook Mistakes to Avoid

Not all attention-grabbing openings are appropriate for academic writing. Avoid these:

  • Too general — "Throughout history, humans have always..." or "In today's world..." These phrases appear in millions of essays and signal that the writer has nothing specific to say.
  • Personal opinion without grounding — "I think social media is very dangerous." This is too informal and too vague for academic writing.
  • Spoiler — Stating your conclusion in the hook. "Social media is harmful and should be banned." This kills the argument before it starts — the reader has no reason to continue.

A strong hook creates a question in the reader's mind — it should make them want to know what comes next, not feel they already do.

Thesis or Announcement?

Which of these is a valid thesis statement? The other is an announcement of intention — a common but weak substitute.

A. "In this essay, I will discuss the effects of climate change on agriculture, water supply, and coastal communities."
B. "Climate change poses an existential threat to global food security, freshwater availability, and the habitability of coastal regions worldwide."

One Sentence Doesn't Belong

This introduction has four sentences — but one of them disrupts the Hook → Background → Thesis structure. Click on it.

"By 2030, more than half the jobs that exist today will be significantly altered or replaced by artificial intelligence, according to the World Economic Forum." "This transformation is already visible in sectors ranging from manufacturing and logistics to legal research and medical diagnosis." "The history of automation dates back to the Industrial Revolution in the 18th century, when machines first began replacing manual labour." "Governments, educators, and businesses must therefore collaborate urgently to reskill the workforce, redesign educational pathways, and establish ethical frameworks for AI deployment."

Writing Task Instructions

You will write a complete academic introduction. Your paragraph must follow the Hook → Background → Thesis structure. Pay close attention to hook quality: it must be specific, engaging, and appropriate for academic writing. Target length: 80–110 words. Allow approximately 10 minutes. Your writing will be tracked for academic integrity purposes.

Write a complete academic introduction (80–110 words) on the following topic:

"The role of physical education in schools."

Your introduction must include:
1. Hook — a specific, engaging opening (a surprising statistic, a rhetorical question, or a brief anecdote). Do not use "Since the beginning of time" or similar generic phrases.
2. Background — 1–2 sentences that contextualise the topic.
3. Thesis — one sentence that announces your position and three supporting arguments.

The thesis must be the last sentence.
