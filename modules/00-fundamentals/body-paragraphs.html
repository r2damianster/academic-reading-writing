Body Paragraphs

The Paragraph Is Not Alone

A body paragraph does not exist independently. It is a response to one specific point in your thesis statement. The essay has a clear architecture:

📌 Thesis Statement → announces 3 arguments
   ↳ Body Paragraph 1 → develops argument 1
   ↳ Body Paragraph 2 → develops argument 2
   ↳ Body Paragraph 3 → develops argument 3
📌 Conclusion → restates and closes

Each body paragraph has a 1:1 relationship with one point in the thesis. If a paragraph introduces an argument the thesis never mentioned, the essay loses coherence.

Two Sentences Students Confuse

This is one of the most common sources of confusion in academic writing:

Thesis Statement — appears in the introduction. It announces the entire argument of the essay, usually listing all three points.
Example: "Remote work increases productivity, improves well-being, and reduces operational costs for companies."
Topic Sentence — appears at the start of each body paragraph. It announces one specific point that was listed in the thesis.
Example: "One of the most significant advantages of remote work is its positive effect on employee productivity."

The Topic Sentence is essentially the thesis's child — it inherits one argument and expands it.

The Intruder

Thesis: "A plant-based diet improves cardiovascular health, reduces environmental impact, and lowers food costs."

Three of these Topic Sentences respond to the thesis. One does not. Click on the intruder.

"Research consistently shows that diets high in plant-based foods reduce the risk of heart disease and stroke." "Producing plant-based food generates significantly fewer greenhouse gas emissions than livestock farming." "The cultural traditions associated with meat consumption have deep historical roots in most societies." "Staple plant foods such as legumes, grains, and seasonal vegetables are substantially cheaper than meat per kilogram."

The Closing Sentence Does Two Jobs

The final sentence of a body paragraph should not just summarise the point — it should also signal what is coming next. This is called a transition sentence.

A transition sentence does two things at once:

  • ✅ Closes the current paragraph by reinforcing its main point
  • ✅ Bridges to the next paragraph by hinting at the next argument
Closing only: "Therefore, regular exercise significantly reduces the risk of cardiovascular disease."

Closing + Bridge: "Therefore, regular exercise significantly reduces the risk of cardiovascular disease — yet its benefits extend well beyond physical health."

The second version closes the argument and prepares the reader for a paragraph about mental or social benefits.

One Job or Two?

Does this sentence close the paragraph only, or does it also bridge to the next argument?

"Thus, renewable energy sources have proven to be economically viable at scale — a finding that raises an equally important question about their environmental sustainability."

A. It only closes the paragraph about economic viability.
B. It closes the economic argument and bridges to a paragraph about environmental impact.

Closing vs. Bridging

Both sentences close a paragraph about technology improving education access. Notice the difference.

"In conclusion, digital platforms have made quality education accessible to millions of students who previously had no access to it."
"In conclusion, digital platforms have made quality education accessible to millions — though this expansion in access has also introduced new concerns about the quality and reliability of online content."

Writing Task Instructions

You will write a complete body paragraph in response to a given thesis statement. Your paragraph must include a Topic Sentence that directly responds to one point of the thesis, supporting evidence with analysis, and a closing sentence that bridges to the next argument. Allow approximately 10–12 minutes. Your writing will be tracked for academic integrity purposes.

Thesis: "Access to public libraries strengthens communities by promoting literacy, providing free resources, and creating inclusive public spaces."

Write a complete body paragraph (110–150 words) developing the first point: how libraries promote literacy.

Your paragraph must include:
1. A Topic Sentence that responds directly to the thesis.
2. At least one piece of evidence with analysis.
3. A closing sentence that closes your point and bridges to the next argument (free resources).
