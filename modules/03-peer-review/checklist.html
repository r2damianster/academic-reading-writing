The Review Checklist

Review in the Right Order

Not all writing problems carry the same weight — and fixing a lower-priority problem before a higher-priority one wastes effort. If you spend time correcting the grammar of a paragraph that later gets deleted, that work is lost.

Priority 1 — Argument and structure
Does the essay have a clear thesis? Does each paragraph support it? Is the overall structure logical?
Priority 2 — Development of ideas
Is each point sufficiently developed? Is the evidence relevant and analysed, not just summarised?
Priority 3 — Coherence and flow
Do paragraphs connect? Are transitions and connectors accurate and varied?
Priority 4 — Language and vocabulary
Is the register consistently academic? Are grammar and sentence structure correct?
Priority 5 — Format and conventions
Are citations, references, and formatting correct? Is the word count appropriate?

Put the Review Tasks in Order

These five review tasks are listed randomly. Put them in the correct order from most to least important.

Check that the register is consistently academic and that vocabulary is precise throughout.
Read the essay to assess whether the thesis is clear and whether each paragraph advances the central argument.
Verify that citations follow the correct format and that the reference list is complete and accurate.
Check that transitions between paragraphs are logical and that connectors are used accurately.
Evaluate whether the evidence in each paragraph is relevant, sufficient, and analysed rather than simply reported.

Which Priority Does This Belong To?

Drag each writing problem to the review priority it falls under.

The essay has three body paragraphs, but only two of them relate to the thesis.
The conclusion introduces a new argument not developed in the body.
The evidence in paragraph 2 is a direct quote with no explanation of its relevance.
Paragraph 3 makes a claim but provides no supporting evidence.
The essay uses "a lot of" and "really important" throughout.
Three sentences use the wrong tense in the methods section.
The in-text citations do not follow APA 7 format.
The reference list is missing two of the sources cited in the essay.

What Should the Reviewer Address First?

A reviewer is reading a peer's essay and notices the following problems:

— The thesis statement is vague and does not take a clear position
— One paragraph uses informal language ("it's pretty obvious that")
— Two citations are missing from the reference list
— The transition between paragraphs 2 and 3 is weak

What should the reviewer prioritise in their feedback?

A. The vague thesis — this is a structural problem that affects the entire essay and must be resolved before lower-priority issues.
B. The informal language — register errors are visible to the reader immediately.
C. The missing citations — these are an academic integrity issue and must be addressed urgently.
D. All problems have equal priority and should be listed in the order they appear in the essay.

First Things First

In each scenario, choose the problem the writer should address first when revising their own work.

A writer has limited time before submission and finds two problems: a comma splice in paragraph 1 and a body paragraph that does not connect to the thesis.

Fix the comma splice first — grammar errors are penalised in marking. Address the paragraph that doesn't connect to the thesis first — a structural problem is higher priority than a punctuation error.

A peer reviewer notices that the introduction is excellent but the conclusion simply repeats the thesis word for word, and there is also a minor spelling error in the title.

Mention the spelling error first — it is the first thing the reader sees. Prioritise the conclusion — repeating the thesis without synthesis is a structural weakness that affects the quality of the argument. The spelling error can be noted briefly at the end.
