Integrating Visuals

A Visual Cannot Speak for Itself

One of the most common errors in academic writing is inserting a graph or table and assuming it proves the argument on its own. It does not. Every visual requires three moves from the writer:

1. Introduction — tell the reader what they are about to see and why it matters
2. The visual itself — the figure, table, or diagram, labelled correctly
3. Analysis — explain what the visual means in relation to your argument

The introduction and analysis are not optional. A visual without them is decorative, not argumentative. The analysis is the most important step — it is where your voice enters the data.

Introducing a Visual in Academic Writing

The sentence that introduces a visual does two things: it signals what the visual shows and connects it to the argument. There are two standard patterns:

Pattern 1 — Lead with the visual:
"As shown in Figure 1, global plastic production increased by 400% between 1980 and 2020."
Pattern 2 — Lead with the claim:
"Plastic production has grown exponentially over the past four decades (see Figure 1)."
What to avoid:
"The graph below shows information about plastic." — This describes the visual without connecting it to any argument.

In APA format, figures are numbered sequentially (Figure 1, Figure 2…) and tables separately (Table 1, Table 2…).

Integrated vs. Dumped

Both paragraphs include the same bar chart about university dropout rates. One integrates it; the other dumps it.

Financial stress is a significant issue for university students. [Bar chart inserted here.] Many students leave university before completing their degrees.

Problems: no introduction of the figure, no analysis, no connection to the argument. The chart could be removed without changing the paragraph.
Financial stress is a leading driver of university dropout rates. As illustrated in Figure 1, institutions with the highest tuition fees consistently report dropout rates above 30% — more than double the rate at fully subsidised universities. This disparity strongly suggests that economic barriers, rather than academic ability, are the primary cause of non-completion.

The figure is introduced, cited, and then analysed — the writer's argument depends on it.

Description vs. Analysis

This paragraph integrates a line graph about screen time. One sentence merely describes what the graph shows without analysing it — that is the visual evidence dump. Click on it.

The correlation between increased screen time and declining sleep quality among teenagers is clearly illustrated in Figure 3. The line graph shows data from 2015 to 2023, with screen time on the x-axis measured in hours and sleep duration on the y-axis measured in hours per night. Notably, every one-hour increase in daily screen time corresponds to an average reduction of 22 minutes of sleep — a relationship that remains consistent across all age groups studied. This inverse correlation suggests that digital device use is not merely associated with poor sleep but may actively displace it, with direct consequences for adolescent cognitive performance.

Writing Task Instructions

You will write a complete academic paragraph that introduces and analyses a described visual. You will not see an actual graph — instead, the data is described for you. Your task is to write the paragraph as if the graph were present: introduce it, reference it, and — most importantly — analyse what it means for the argument. Allow approximately 10–12 minutes. Your writing will be tracked for academic integrity purposes.

The visual (described): A bar chart (Figure 1) comparing the percentage of students who report high levels of academic stress in three university systems: fully online (67%), hybrid (48%), and fully in-person (39%). Data from a 2023 international survey of 12,000 university students.

Your argument: Online learning, despite its flexibility, generates higher levels of academic stress than traditional formats.

Write a paragraph (110–150 words) that:
1. States the argument in a Topic Sentence.
2. Introduces Figure 1 with a proper referencing phrase.
3. Describes the key finding (briefly — one sentence).
4. Analyses what the data means for the argument (this is the most important part).
5. Closes with a sentence that connects back to the main claim.

Do not simply list what the chart shows. Your analysis must explain why the pattern exists or what it implies.
