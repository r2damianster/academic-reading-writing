Nominalisation

The Engine of Academic Prose

Nominalisation is the process of converting a verb or adjective into a noun. It is the single most defining feature of formal academic register — and the most powerful tool for compressing and chaining arguments.

What it does:

(1) Compresses information:
"The government implemented the policy" → "The implementation of the policy"
Now that whole event is a noun — you can use it as a subject, object, or in a prepositional phrase.

(2) Creates argumentative chains:
"The government implemented the policy. The implementation proved controversial. This controversy led to a review. The review revealed..."
Each nominalisation becomes the subject of the next sentence — the argument flows forward.
What it signals:

Nominalisation creates distance between the writer and the event — it makes processes and states feel objective and established, rather than performed by a person.

"We analysed the data" → personal, conversational
"The analysis of the data" → impersonal, academic

This is not evasion — it is the conventional register of academic argument.

Five Suffixes to Master

Most nominalisations are formed by adding one of five suffixes. Knowing these patterns lets you nominalise any verb or adjective on the spot:

-tion / -sion
implement → implementation
conclude → conclusion
discuss → discussion
-ment
develop → development
achieve → achievement
assess → assessment
-ance / -ence
perform → performance
differ → difference
persist → persistence
-ity
complex → complexity
stable → stability
available → availability
-al
approve → approval / arrive → arrival / remove → removal
High-frequency irregulars: strong → strength / long → length / grow → growth / choose → choice / lose → loss / prove → proof
