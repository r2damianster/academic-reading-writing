Passive Voice in Academic Writing

Not a Rule — A Choice

The passive is not inherently academic. It is a tool for controlling focus. Use it when the object, process, or result matters more than who performed the action.

✅ Use passive when:

(1) The agent is unknown: "The manuscript was written in the 14th century."
(2) The agent is irrelevant: "The samples were stored at −20°C." (who stored them doesn't matter)
(3) The object/process is the focus: "Three hypotheses were tested." (not who tested them)
(4) Disciplinary convention: sciences and social sciences use passive in methods sections to signal objectivity
❌ Avoid passive when:

(1) The agent matters for the argument: "It was decided that funding would be cut" — who decided? This hides accountability.
(2) It creates ambiguity: "The report was reviewed and the policy was changed" — by whom? Are they the same actor?
(3) It produces excessive nominalisations: "A determination was made by the committee" → "The committee determined" is cleaner.

Three Tenses You Need

The passive is not one construction — it varies by tense. These three cover 90% of academic usage:

Simple Past Passive — was / were + past participle
Used in: methods, literature review (what was done)
"Data was collected over a six-month period."
"Participants were recruited from three urban hospitals."
Present Perfect Passive — has / have been + past participle
Used in: literature review (what the field has established)
"This relationship has been demonstrated across multiple disciplines."
"The model has been applied in over forty empirical studies."
Modal Passive — modal + be + past participle
Used in: recommendations, conclusions, hedged claims
"These findings should be interpreted with caution."
"Further research must be conducted before policy implications can be drawn."

Choose the Right Voice

Read the context carefully. The correct choice depends on what the writer needs to foreground.

Context: A methods section. The writer wants to foreground the procedure, not the researcher.

We interviewed forty participants using a semi-structured protocol. Forty participants were interviewed using a semi-structured protocol.

Context: An argument about political accountability. The agent is the main point.

The Treasury unilaterally decided to redirect the ring-fenced funds without parliamentary approval. The ring-fenced funds were redirected without parliamentary approval.

Context: A conclusion with a hedged recommendation.

Researchers should conduct further studies before drawing policy conclusions. Further studies should be conducted before policy conclusions can be drawn.

Write a methods paragraph (100–130 words) for the following hypothetical study:

A mixed-methods study examining the relationship between smartphone use before sleep and academic performance among university students. The study used a survey instrument administered to 200 students, followed by semi-structured interviews with 15 participants selected from the survey respondents.

Requirements:
— Use simple past passive for completed procedural steps (was administered / were selected / were conducted)
— Use modal passive for at least one methodological justification (should be noted / must be acknowledged)
— Use active voice at least once — where the agent is relevant to the argument
— Do not use first person (I / we) — maintain impersonal academic register
