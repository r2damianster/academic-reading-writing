Supporting Sentences

What Are Supporting Sentences?

Once your Topic Sentence makes a claim, the supporting sentences are the proof and development of that claim. There are five main types:

  • Example — a specific case that illustrates the point
  • Statistic — a number or data point from research
  • Quote — the exact words of an expert or study
  • Anecdote — a brief real-life story that shows the point in action
  • Definition — clarifying a key term used in the claim

A strong paragraph usually combines more than one type — but each sentence must connect to the same central idea.

The Chain Rule

Supporting sentences must form a chain, not a pile. Each sentence should connect logically to the one before it — not jump to a new idea.

Think of it as building a wall: every brick must sit on the previous one. If you skip a brick, the wall collapses.

Ask yourself after each sentence: "Does this follow naturally from what I just said?"

Linking Signals

Good supporting sentences use linking language to make the chain visible to the reader. Some of the most useful signals are:

  • For instance / For example — to introduce a specific case
  • Specifically — to narrow or zoom in on the point
  • This is evident in... — to connect evidence back to the claim
  • Furthermore / In addition — to add a new but related point
  • This suggests / This indicates — to begin your analysis of evidence

Notice: these signals tell the reader what kind of sentence is coming next before they even read it.

Chain vs. Pile

Topic Sentence: "Remote work has improved employee well-being."

Supporting: For instance, a 2022 Microsoft survey found that 73% of remote workers reported lower stress levels.
Analysis: This is significant because it suggests that eliminating the daily commute directly reduces daily pressure.
Development: Furthermore, workers reported having more time for personal routines, which reinforced their sense of control.
Fact 1: Many companies moved to remote work in 2020.
Fact 2: Zoom became a popular platform.
Fact 3: Home offices require good internet connections.
Fact 4: Some people prefer working in cafés.
