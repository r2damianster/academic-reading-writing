Conditionals in Academic Writing

Conditionals in Academic Argument

Conditionals are not just grammar — they encode the writer's relationship to evidence, possibility, and counterfactual reasoning. Each type signals a different epistemic stance:

ZERO — Universal truth / scientific law
If + present simple → present simple
"If temperature rises, ice melts."
Use for established principles, definitions, and laws. The result always follows the condition.
FIRST — Real / likely prediction
If + present simple → will + infinitive
"If funding is maintained, the programme will produce measurable outcomes by 2027."
Use for predictions based on evidence where the condition is realistic.
SECOND — Hypothetical / theoretical
If + past simple → would + infinitive
"If this model were accurate, it would predict a non-linear relationship between the variables."
Use for theoretical reasoning, hypotheses, and scenarios the writer considers unlikely or unverified. Note: were (not was) for all persons in formal writing.
THIRD — Counterfactual / past hypothetical
If + past perfect → would have + past participle
"If the intervention had been implemented earlier, the outcomes would have been substantially different."
Use for retrospective analysis — what would have happened under different conditions. Common in policy analysis and historical argument.

The Second Conditional: Academic Rigour, Not Doubt

The second conditional is systematically underused by academic writers who confuse it with personal uncertainty. In academic argument, it signals something quite different:

Testing a hypothesis:
"If Bourdieu's model were fully applicable here, we would expect habitus to override individual rational choice entirely — yet the data does not support this prediction."
→ The writer is not expressing doubt. They are using the second conditional to set up a logical test of the theory.

Proposing a theoretical scenario:
"If universal basic income were implemented at scale, labour market participation patterns would shift in ways that current models cannot fully predict."
→ The writer is not saying UBI is impossible. They are reasoning about an as-yet-unrealised scenario.

⚠️ In formal academic writing, use were for all persons in second conditional if-clauses — not was. "If this were true..." not "If this was true..."

Which Type Fits the Logic?

The correct conditional depends on the writer's epistemic relationship to the condition. Read carefully.

Context: Discussing a well-established biological principle.

If a cell is deprived of oxygen, it begins to die within minutes. were deprived of oxygen, it would begin to die within minutes.

Context: Testing whether a theoretical model holds against empirical data.

If the rational choice framework is correct, we will observe consistent utility-maximising behaviour across all income groups. were correct, we would observe consistent utility-maximising behaviour across all income groups — a pattern the data does not support.

Context: Counterfactual analysis of a past policy failure.

If stricter regulatory oversight had been in place, the financial collapse of 2008 would have been significantly less severe. were in place, the financial collapse of 2008 would be significantly less severe.

Order the Hypothetical Argument

These four sentences build a conditional argument. Put them in the order that makes the logical progression clearest.

The model predicts a strong positive correlation between institutional trust and civic participation.
If this prediction were accurate, we would expect countries with higher trust scores to show significantly higher voter turnout.
The data, however, reveals no such pattern across the 28 countries examined.
If the model had accounted for structural barriers to participation — registration requirements, electoral system design — its predictive validity would have been substantially stronger.
